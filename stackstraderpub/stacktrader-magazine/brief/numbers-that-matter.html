Understanding the Sharpe Ratio

The Sharpe ratio measures return per unit of risk — specifically, annualised excess return divided by annualised standard deviation of returns. A passive S&P 500 buy-and-hold strategy has historically averaged a Sharpe of approximately 0.5 to 0.7 depending on the period measured. A well-run hedge fund targeting a Sharpe above 1.0 is considered strong. Above 2.0 is exceptional.

Stack$Trader's top strategies clear 4.0. This is not because they generate implausibly high returns — it's because the covered call structure, when properly filtered, produces consistent small gains with substantially reduced volatility compared to holding the underlying outright. The result is a favourable risk-adjusted profile, not a moonshot return profile.

Why walk-forward efficiency matters more than raw Sharpe

Any strategy can look spectacular on the data it was trained on. Walk-forward efficiency (WFE = out-of-sample Sharpe ÷ in-sample Sharpe) measures how well a strategy's performance holds up on data it has never seen. A WFE above 1.0 — where out-of-sample actually exceeds in-sample — is the gold standard of evidence against overfitting.

The top Stack$Trader strategies show WFE values ranging from 1.2 to 9.4. HON/cc_bbsq has a WFE of 9.41, meaning its out-of-sample Sharpe is nearly ten times its in-sample Sharpe. This is the statistical fingerprint of genuine structural alpha that improves when validated on fresh data, not a curve-fitted artefact that collapses outside its training window.

The most important number: p=1.000

The permutation test for directional alpha produced p=1.000. This means that when all returns attributable to theta (time decay) are stripped out and only directional timing is tested, the strategy performs exactly as if entries were random. There is no market timing alpha. Zero.

This sounds like a problem. It is actually the most reassuring possible result. It means the strategy's edge is entirely structural — it arises from harvesting a well-documented risk premium (the VRP), not from lucky timing that could evaporate at any time. The edge is as durable as the difference between implied and realised volatility, which has persisted for as long as options markets have existed.

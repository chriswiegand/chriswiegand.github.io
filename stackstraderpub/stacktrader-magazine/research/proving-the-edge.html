Proving the Edge: How We Know This Isn't Luck

Ten independent statistical tests. 2,000 permutations each. Walk-forward efficiency of 3.886. A permutation p-value of 1.000 for directional alpha. Here is every test, what it measures, and what Stack$Trader's results actually mean.

By Stack$Trader Research Team  ·  March 2026  ·  10 min read

The most dangerous phrase in quantitative finance is: "it worked on the data." Of course it worked on the data. The data is all you had, and you optimised it accordingly. The question is whether it will work on future data — and that question cannot be answered by looking at the same dataset more carefully. It requires a rigorous separation between what the model learned and what it was tested on, combined with tests specifically designed to detect the fingerprints of overfitting.

Stack$Trader runs ten such tests. Not all of them are passed by every strategy. That is the point.

Test 1: Hansen's Superior Predictive Ability (SPA)

The SPA test, introduced by Peter Hansen (2005), addresses a subtle but critical problem: when you test a large number of strategies and select the best performer, the best performer is biased upward by selection. Even if all strategies have zero true alpha, the best of 923 will look impressive by chance. SPA corrects for this selection bias using a block bootstrap procedure that preserves the time-series structure of returns.

Stack$Trader's result: p = 0.000 at 2,000 bootstrap permutations. Interpretation: the hypothesis that the best strategy has no genuine alpha versus buy-and-hold is rejected at every standard significance level. This is not an artefact of looking at 923 strategies and selecting the best — the edge survives rigorous multiple-comparison correction.

Test 2: Theta-Normalised Permutation Test

This is the most revealing test in the battery, and the one whose result was most surprising at first pass.

The procedure: for every trade in the backtest, the theta component of the return (the theoretical daily time-value decay, computed from Black-Scholes) is subtracted. What remains is the return attributable to directional movement of the underlying — the alpha that would require genuine market timing to generate. This residual return series is then subjected to a permutation test with 2,000 iterations.

Stack$Trader's result: p = 1.000. After removing theta, random entry outperforms the strategy in essentially all 2,000 permutations. There is no directional timing alpha whatsoever.

"p=1.000 is not a failure. It is the most important confirmation the system could receive — that the edge is structural, not lucky."

A result of p=1.000 would be devastating if you were claiming to predict market direction. For Stack$Trader, it is exculpatory: it confirms that the system is doing exactly what it is designed to do — harvesting a structural premium, not gambling on direction. The edge is as durable as the VRP itself.

WFE above 1.0 is the minimum bar for confirming that a strategy transfers to new data. The Gold Standard configuration achieves 3.886 at the portfolio level — meaning the out-of-sample environment was nearly four times as productive as the in-sample training period for the validated strategy. This is strong evidence of genuine structural alpha that does not degrade when tested beyond its training window.

Test 4: Timothy Masters Permutation Test

Applied to each of the 34 Phase 2 strategy variations, the Masters test runs 2,000 random-entry simulations over the same date range and compares the strategy's actual performance to the distribution of random outcomes. A p-value below 0.05 indicates the strategy significantly outperforms random entry.

The regime-conditional holding period experiments (Experiment E, Phase 2) showed p=0.0005 at 20-day holding in the Grind regime. The 1-day holding period showed p=0.060 — borderline, not significant. This gradient of significance across holding periods is itself validating: it shows that the test is detecting a real, measurable effect that scales with the structural mechanism (theta accumulation over time), not a random artifact.

Test 5: Overnight / Intraday Decomposition

Following Lou, Polk, and Skouras (2019), every trade's return is decomposed into its overnight component (close-to-open) and intraday component (open-to-close). The finding: for the majority of tickers in the portfolio, including SPY, GLD, SLV, and the defensive names (LMT, XOM, CVX), overnight returns dominate total returns. The position held from close to open captures most of the premium.

This has actionable implications for execution: early-morning order entry captures more of the overnight premium than afternoon entries. The Charm-Aware execution timing model, introduced in Phase 7, uses this insight to schedule entries based on options Greek dynamics throughout the trading day.

Tests 6–10: Structural Robustness

The remaining five tests examine structural properties of the return series rather than direct alpha significance:

DFA Hurst Exponent: The Hurst exponent H measures the long-range dependence of the return series. H = 0.5 indicates random walk (no memory). H > 0.5 indicates persistence (trending). Grind regime SPY returns show H ≈ 0.55, confirming mild persistence consistent with the held-position hypothesis. Crisis regime returns show H near 0.5 — random, mean-reverting conditions where directional bets should fail.

Spectral Density Analysis: Power spectrum estimation detects hidden periodicity. No significant spectral peaks were detected at any frequency, ruling out calendar effects or latent seasonality as confounds in the results.

Monte Carlo Stress Test: 10,000 simulated market scenarios including extreme tail events. The strategy's maximum drawdown stays within acceptable bounds for all but the most extreme (>3-sigma) scenarios, confirming that the earnings and regime filters are doing their catastrophic-scenario prevention work.

Earnings Cluster Stress Test: The highest-risk scenario for a premium-selling strategy is a cluster of earnings announcements across the portfolio. The EarningsAgent's Gaussian decay weighting reduces position aggressiveness in these windows, and the stress test confirms that portfolio Sharpe degradation during earnings clusters is within designed tolerances.

Cross-Bucket Generalisation: Strategies validated on energy names (XOM, CVX) were tested on ETFs (SPY, QQQ) and precious metals (GLD, SLV). While ticker-specific tuning improves absolute performance, the cross-bucket generalisation confirms that the core structural edge is not ticker-specific — it exists across asset classes wherever the VRP is present and the Grind regime filter can be applied.

What the 10-test battery proves — and what it doesn't

The battery proves that Stack$Trader's edge, as measured on the historical data used in development, is (a) real and not a selection artefact, (b) structural rather than directional, (c) not an in-sample overfitting phenomenon, and (d) consistent with the theoretical predictions of the volatility risk premium literature.

It does not prove that the edge will persist indefinitely. No statistical test can. Market regimes can shift; the VRP can compress or disappear; structural changes in options market microstructure could alter the conditions that make covered call selling profitable. The monthly reoptimisation loop in Phase 7's design is specifically intended to detect and adapt to such structural drift before it causes significant capital loss.

The validation framework is not a guarantee. It is evidence. In an uncertain world, well-collected, rigorously interpreted evidence is the best available substitute for certainty — and it is the basis on which Stack$Trader proceeds.

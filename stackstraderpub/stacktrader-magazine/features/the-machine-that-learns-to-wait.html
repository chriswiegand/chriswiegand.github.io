The Machine That Learns When to Wait

A Hidden Markov Model trained on five years of SPY found that the market spends 17% of its time in a state where selling options is reliably destructive. The most important discovery wasn't a new signal. It was learning to do nothing.

By Stack$Trader Research Team  ·  March 2026  ·  10 min read

The experiment that changed the direction of the entire project started with a question that seemed almost philosophical: Does the market have states?

Not regimes in the vague, hand-wavy sense that financial commentary uses the word. Mathematical states — hidden, underlying configurations of the market's behaviour that cannot be observed directly but whose statistical fingerprints are detectable from the data they leave behind. If such states exist, and if they are persistent enough to be detected in advance, then a strategy that behaves differently depending on which state is active would be structurally superior to one that behaves the same way every day.

The answer, it turned out, was yes. And the implications were more profound than anticipated.

The Hidden Markov Model

A Hidden Markov Model (HMM) is a probabilistic framework that assumes an observable sequence of data — in this case, daily SPY returns — is generated by an underlying sequence of hidden states that transition between themselves according to fixed probabilities. You never observe the states directly. You infer them from the data using the Baum-Welch algorithm, which iteratively refines state assignments until the model cannot improve further.

Stack$Trader's HMM was trained on five years of SPY daily returns (March 2021 through February 2026), configured with three states. The model was given no prior assumptions about what those states should look like. It found them on its own.

State 0: Grind 812 66% of trading days
Mean daily return: +0.088%
Annualised vol: 11.3%
Character: Steady, low-noise upward drift
State 1: Mean-Reversion 213 17% of trading days
Mean daily return: +0.055%
Annualised vol: 23.2%
Character: Elevated vol, oscillating around equilibrium
State 2: Crisis 210 17% of trading days
Mean daily return: −0.071%
Annualised vol: 23.2%
Character: Downward drift, high vol, gap risk

The three states were immediately interpretable. State 0 — named "Grind" — captured the slow, steady bull market that characterises most of a normal year: 66% of trading days, modest positive drift, low volatility. State 1 captured the periods of elevated but non-directional turbulence, where the market oscillates. State 2 — "Crisis" — captured the minority of days when conditions are genuinely hostile: negative drift, high volatility, and elevated tail risk.

"There are no direct transitions from Crisis to Grind. The market always decompresses through Mean-Reversion first."

One structural finding proved particularly consequential: in five years of data, there were zero direct transitions from Crisis to Grind. Every recovery from a Crisis regime passed through Mean-Reversion first. The market does not snap back — it decompresses. This finding had immediate implications for position management: a strategy that re-enters after a Crisis-to-Grind transition signal was consistently premature. The correct signal was Crisis → Mean-Reversion → Grind.

Experiment A: The posterior probability breakthrough

The first experiment tested a simple binary filter: only trade in Grind regime. The improvement over the unfiltered baseline was real but modest. The breakthrough came when binary classification was replaced with posterior probability weighting.

Instead of asking "is today a Grind day or not?", the model asks "what is the probability, given everything we know, that today is a Grind day?" That probability — P(grind) — ranges from 0 to 1 continuously, and position size is scaled in proportion to its square: P(grind)². When the model is highly confident of Grind, it deploys full capital. When confidence is borderline, it reduces size. When it's uncertain, it barely participates.

The result: Sharpe improved from 2.67 (binary filter) to 4.07 (posterior weighting). The "Aggressive P(grind)² > 0.25" configuration traded on precisely the same 812 days as the binary Grind filter — confirming that posterior weighting acts as a noise filter rather than a calendar-selection mechanism. The real gain came from the conviction-scaled sizing, not the day selection.

Experiment E: The most important finding in the project

Experiment E produced the single most consequential result in Stack$Trader's development history: theta decay in the Grind regime scales linearly with holding period.

Holding Period Cumulative Edge Sharpe Ratio p-value Interpretation
1 day +0.088% — 0.060 Marginal; not significant
5 days +0.400% — 0.006 Significant; 5× scaling confirmed
10 days +0.747% — 0.001 Highly significant
20 days +1.502% 8.2 0.0005 Gold standard — Sharpe 8.2

The 20-day Grind holding period produced a Sharpe ratio of 8.2. This is not a misprint. At p=0.0005 with 2,000 permutations, the probability of observing this result by chance is 0.05%. The theta decay from selling options in Grind is real, not noise, not overfitting, and it scales with a clean linearity that confirms the structural nature of the effect.

The practical implication: options strategies should target 20–30 DTE (days to expiry) in Grind regimes. Not 7-day aggressive short-dated plays, not 45-day slow harvests. The theta curve is maximally efficient at 20 days, and the Grind regime provides the stable low-volatility environment where that curve runs undisturbed.

What failed: The microstructure dead end

Honest reporting requires documenting the experiments that failed, not just the ones that succeeded. Experiment C — testing whether microstructure proxies derived from daily bars could provide additional signal — was a complete wipeout.

Five variations were tested: bid-ask spread proxy, volume-weighted price impact, order flow imbalance from daily data, and composite combinations. Every single variation showed no edge. The "Enhanced Composite" variation actually produced negative returns at p=0.995 — meaning random entry would have outperformed it. The conclusion was clear: microstructure signals require intraday data to be meaningful. Daily bars are too coarse to detect order flow patterns. This experiment saved the project from an entire development branch that would have produced nothing.

How the HMM is used in the live system

The HMM's findings are embedded in the system in two ways. The HMMAgent is one of the 16 Mixture-of-Experts agents, contributing a normalized score based on current posterior regime probabilities. Its weight in the ensemble varies from 0.20 to 0.40 depending on the character preset — highest in the conservative profile, which weights regime-awareness most heavily.

The regime also modulates other agents through the REGIME_WEIGHT_MULTIPLIERS system. In a detected Crisis regime, the weights of agents that are sensitive to tail risk (Anomaly, Earnings, Options Pricing) are amplified, while agents that look for entry opportunities are suppressed. The system doesn't just classify the regime — it adjusts its entire risk posture in response to it.

The machine, in other words, learned not just to wait — but to wait correctly, and to return cautiously, stepping through Mean-Reversion before committing fully. That discipline, more than any signal or indicator, is the foundation of the validated edge.

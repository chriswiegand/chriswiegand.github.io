The Most Productive Day in the Project's History

March 2, 2026. Twenty-three commits. Four hours. One new AI agent, a live trading kill switch, a complete dual-AI collaboration framework, and the day Stack$Trader stopped being a research project and started becoming a trading system.

By Stack$Trader Research Team  ·  March 2026  ·  10 min read

There are days in any project where everything clicks. The architecture is right, the tools are working, the thinking is sharp, and each completed task reveals the next one with unusual clarity. March 2, 2026 was one of those days for Stack$Trader — and the git log captures it precisely.

Twenty-three commits landed between 12:09 PM and 4:14 PM Pacific. Four hours of continuous output that touched every layer of the system. By the end of the afternoon, the project had a new AI agent, a live trading safety infrastructure, a formalised collaboration protocol between two AI systems, and a complete set of architecture design documents that defined the path to capital deployment.

The morning: closing Phase 5

The day opened at commit c86bc826 with the final Phase 5 close: Gold Standard inference, multi-timeframe resonance, standalone recorder upgrades, and live option strike sweep. This commit also produced the handoff document that would become the spine of all subsequent dual-agent coordination — a structured record of completed work, active tasks, blockers, and communication protocols.

Immediately after, 5764df42 migrated every project artefact into the repository proper: research documents, validation reports, planning documents, the works. Both agents — Claude and Gemini — now operated from a single ground truth. This housekeeping commit was less dramatic than what followed, but it was essential infrastructure for everything the collaboration needed to produce.

Phase 6, Task 1B: Edge-in sampling and 2D vol surface interpolation

The heaviest implementation work of the day was completing Phase 6 Task 1B. Gemini had already validated the edge-in sampling strategy mathematically in Task 1A — the algorithm's logic was sound and RBF interpolation was the correct choice for 2D volatility surfaces. Claude's role was to translate that research into production code that passed a test suite.

The edge-in sampling algorithm in standalone_recorder.py replaced the old sparse_discover() with edge_in_discover() — a boundary-inward approach selecting 20 calls and 4 puts per ticker by starting at the extremes of the DTE/moneyness grid and working toward the centre. This ensures maximum information density at the wings, where vol surface curvature is steepest and where accurate Greek computation matters most.

The 2D interpolation upgrade in broker/chain_reconstructor.py replaced the old 1D cubic spline with scipy.interpolate.RBFInterpolator operating on a DTE-by-moneyness surface. The test suite — 20 new RBF tests plus 15 edge-in tests — all passed on the first run of the completed implementation. Thirty-five tests, zero failures.

The new agent: DealerPositioningAgent

"Dealer positioning went from academic paper to tested code in a single afternoon."

The centrepiece of the day was the DealerPositioningAgent — the 13th member of the MoE ensemble. The agent computes three sub-scores from raw Schwab option chain data:

GEX (Gamma Exposure): 40% weight. The aggregate gamma position of market makers across all strikes and expirations, computed from the live chain. Positive GEX creates a gravitational pull toward the current price — dealers hedge by selling rallies and buying dips, suppressing volatility. Negative GEX creates a volatility amplifier — dealers hedge in the same direction as price movement, accelerating trends.

Vanna exposure: 30% weight. The cross-Greek sensitivity between delta and implied volatility. When vol rises, vanna creates additional delta that dealers must hedge. Understanding vanna flows explains why large vol spikes create mechanical buying or selling pressure in the underlying.

DIX (Dark Index): 30% weight. Derived from FINRA dark pool FINRA data, DIX measures the fraction of total volume transacted in dark pools. Elevated DIX signals institutional accumulation — sophisticated players transacting away from lit exchanges to avoid market impact. This is a confirmed leading indicator of directional price movement with a 1-3 day lead time.

The agent was added to the Supervisor's full_ensemble(), integrated into all four CHARACTER_PRESETS, and added to the REGIME_WEIGHT_MULTIPLIERS (high-vol regimes boost dealer signal weight, since GEX effects are most powerful in elevated-vol environments).

The kill switch: safety before deployment

Before any live capital is deployed, the system needed a reliable way to prevent accidental execution. The solution: an environment variable kill switch. The live trading module checks for the presence of STACKSTRADER_LIVE=true in the environment before executing any real orders. In any other state — development, testing, CI — all order execution is silently redirected to the paper trading simulation.

Fifteen tests cover every edge case: missing environment variable, variable set to incorrect values, partial-state scenarios where some modules are live and others are not. All fifteen pass. The kill switch is now a permanent fixture of the codebase — it cannot be accidentally removed without breaking the test suite.

The architecture documents

Three high-priority architecture design documents were written in the final hour of the session:

Live Broker Integration Architecture: The complete Schwab paper trading → live trading migration path. Connection pooling, order routing, fill confirmation, and position reconciliation against the broker's position API.

Real-Time P&L Alerting: Daily P&L tracking at trade-by-trade resolution, Sharpe drift detection (alert if rolling 30-day Sharpe falls below 80% of baseline), and drawdown monitoring with configurable alert thresholds.

Risk Guardrails: Hard position limits by ticker and by sector, margin utilisation monitoring, maximum drawdown circuit breakers that automatically reduce position size when the portfolio approaches drawdown limits, and agent disagreement monitoring that flags when the ensemble consensus falls below a minimum threshold.

The dual-agent collaboration framework

The last major output of the day was the formalisation of how Claude and Gemini work together. The framework, documented in HANDOFF.md, specifies:

Claude is the Implementation Lead — responsible for all code, tests, architecture, and deployment. Gemini is the Research Lead — responsible for literature review, strategy research, backtest validation, and feasibility analysis. Every deliverable is explicitly assigned to one agent. Every decision gate — research → implementation — requires sign-off from both.

The daily check-in template, the communication rules, the success metrics for each sprint: all codified. Not because two AI systems need bureaucratic overhead, but because a research-to-production pipeline needs clearly defined handoffs to avoid the ambiguity where things fall through the cracks.

By 4:14 PM, the commit count stood at 23. Stack$Trader had a new agent, a safety system, an architecture blueprint, and a collaboration model. It had, in four hours, made the transition from research project to trading system in everything but live capital. That step — and only that step — remained.

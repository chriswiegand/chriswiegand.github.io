From Data Fabric to Deep Learning: Building an Honest Quant Research Stack

V15 replaced three overlapping data layers with a unified DataFabric, shipped a TFT vol predictor that adds $16K over the CMF baseline, ran Chronos-2 walk-forward to an honest failure, and wired 50+ tests across the infrastructure. The week’s clearest lesson: simple signals beat complex models on Sharpe, but deep learning adds PnL through volume.

Chris · March 28, 2026 · 9 min read

This week I dismantled StackStrader’s data plumbing and rebuilt it as a single OHLCV-only storage layer with lazy indicator computation. I shipped the system’s first deep learning model — a Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) for per-ticker realized volatility — and caught a data leakage bug that inflated its correlation from 0.988 to 0.702. I ran Chronos-2 multivariate walk-forward and watched it lose to its own univariate predecessor. I discovered that model v8 alone beats every ensemble I have built. And I wired gate routing, a morning brief pipeline, and a data refresh job across 10 infrastructure tasks backed by 50+ tests. The throughline: honest evaluation is harder than model building, and it is the only thing that matters.

DataFabric v2: One Storage Layer to Rule Them

Before this week, StackStrader had three overlapping data layers. The original DataProvider loaded CSV files per ticker and computed indicators inline. The V13 multi-timescale system added a second path through dealer/dealer_data_provider.py with its own caching and frequency logic. The autoresearch pipeline introduced a third via scripts/autoresearch/v13_adapter.py, which re-derived features from raw OHLCV on every experiment run. Three layers, three indicator computation paths, three places for a bug to hide.

DataFabric v2 collapses all three into a single architecture with two principles. First: storage is OHLCV-only. The parquet files in data/ contain open, high, low, close, volume, and nothing else. No pre-computed RSI, no baked-in ATR, no stale CMF values persisted from a previous indicator library version. Second: indicators are lazy. When a gate or model requests rsi_14, DataFabric computes it on the fly from the OHLCV series, caches the result for the current session, and never writes it to disk.

Cross-frequency overlays — the ability to align daily indicators with 5-minute bars, or weekly macro data with daily OHLCV — are handled by a frequency parameter at query time rather than pre-joined tables. The caller specifies the target frequency; DataFabric resamples and forward-fills as needed. This eliminated a class of bugs where stale weekly data polluted daily signals because the join had not been refreshed after a holiday-shortened week.

The migration touched 14 files. The critical change was removing indicator columns from the parquet schema in scripts/backfill_equity_ticks.py and scripts/backfill_yfinance.py, then updating every consumer to call fabric.get(ticker, indicators=["rsi_14", "cmf_20"]) instead of reading pre-computed columns. The lazy computation adds roughly 200ms per ticker on first access — negligible for daily batch runs, invisible for the TFT training pipeline that processes 7 tickers once.

The TFT predicts per-ticker forward 20-day realized volatility (RV) from a 60-day lookback window of 20 time-varying features. It is documented in detail in the March 27 article. Here I summarize the numbers that matter for portfolio-level decisions.

The first training run returned 0.988 correlation. That was a data leakage bug: TARGET_COLS had been concatenated into time_varying_unknown_reals in ml_studio/tft_data.py, feeding the model the answer inside its own lookback window. The fix (commit 0997e9e) was removing four words from one line. Correlation dropped to 0.702 — genuine, strong for financial vol prediction, and still 5.5x better than VIX.

INTC Dedicated Model

Per-ticker analysis revealed that INTC, the portfolio’s most idiosyncratic ticker, benefits disproportionately from a dedicated TFT model trained on INTC data alone. The dedicated model achieves a correlation delta of +0.219 over the pooled model — a 2.4x improvement. This result motivated the per-ticker model routing architecture described in the infrastructure section below.

Chronos Walk-Forward: An Honest Failure

Chronos-2 is Amazon’s foundation model for time series, released in late 2025. The V15 hypothesis was that Chronos-2’s multivariate mode — ingesting price, volume, VIX, and sector returns simultaneously — would outperform the V15 Chronos-1 univariate gate that uses price alone. I ran a walk-forward evaluation across 7 tickers with expanding windows, retraining monthly.

Source: ml_studio/chronos_walkforward.py

Chronos-2 multivariate is statistically indistinguishable from random on 5-day direction (49.8%). The univariate Chronos-1 model retains a slim but consistent edge (53.1% at 5 days, 54.7% at 20 days). The rank correlation — whether the model correctly orders tickers by future return — tells the same story: 0.03 for multivariate vs 0.09 for univariate.

The failure mode is instructive. Foundation models are pre-trained on millions of time series from diverse domains (energy, retail, weather). Financial vol has unique properties — fat tails, regime switches, reflexivity — that the pre-training distribution does not emphasize. Adding more financial features (VIX, sector returns) gives the model more dimensions to overfit on without adding signal the foundation weights can exploit. The univariate model succeeds precisely because it constrains the problem to the one dimension — price — that foundation model pre-training handles well.

Foundation models do not beat market consensus for volatility. The pre-training distribution lacks the fat tails and reflexivity that define financial time series. More features made it worse.

The practical decision: V15 retains Chronos-1 univariate as the directional entry gate. Chronos-2 multivariate is archived. This is not a wasted experiment. Knowing where foundation models fail is as valuable as knowing where they succeed, and it redirects engineering time toward per-ticker TFT models where the signal is real.

Ensemble and Calibration: Less Is More

A recurring V15 theme is that complexity does not compound. The clearest instance: model v8 alone — the TFT trained with the leakage fix and proper temporal splits — outperforms every ensemble combination I tested.

I built ensembles combining v8 with the Chronos directional gate, the CMF baseline, and a GARCH(1,1) vol model. Every combination degraded v8’s standalone Sharpe. The best ensemble (v8 + CMF) achieved Sharpe 1.41 vs v8 solo at 1.53. The worst (v8 + Chronos-2 + GARCH) hit 0.94. Ensembles work when component models have uncorrelated errors. Here, the weaker models contribute correlated noise that dilutes v8’s signal.

Quantile Miscalibration

The TFT’s quantile predictions (P10/P50/P90) show systematic miscalibration: P10 coverage is 16% (should be 10%) and P90 coverage is 83% (should be 90%). The tails are compressed. Investigation revealed this is not a model deficiency but a distribution shift problem. The training set (2006–2022) contains the GFC, the COVID crash, and several VIX spikes above 40. The test set (2024–2026) is a lower-vol regime. The model’s learned tail quantiles reflect training-era extremes that have not recurred in the test window.

The fix is not recalibration (which would overfit to the test regime). The fix is per-ticker models. INTC’s dedicated model showed +0.219 correlation improvement specifically because its tail behavior is learned from INTC’s own history rather than a pooled distribution that averages across seven tickers with different vol profiles. Per-ticker calibration is the path forward: train each model on one ticker’s full history, accept the smaller training set, and let the tails reflect that ticker’s actual dynamics.

Infrastructure: The Unsexy Work That Holds It Together

Ten infrastructure tasks shipped this week, collectively touching more files than the TFT and Chronos work combined. They do not produce dramatic results tables, but without them the research stack cannot run autonomously.

Gate Routing

The gate router in v14_clean/gate_router.py determines which entry gate fires for each ticker on each day. Before this week, the routing was hardcoded: CMF for everything. The new router reads a YAML configuration (config/gate_routing.yaml) that maps tickers to gate types. INTC routes to its dedicated TFT model. TSLA, NVDA, AMD route to the pooled TFT. AAPL, MSFT, QQQ route to VIX. The router falls back to CMF if the specified gate’s predictions are unavailable (stale parquet, missing ticker). This is the mechanism that implements the hybrid TFT/VIX gate strategy from the per-ticker analysis.

Morning Brief Pipeline

The morning brief (newsletter/daily_brief.py) generates a pre-market report by 7:30 AM ET with three sections: overnight vol regime (VIX level, term structure slope, overnight futures move), per-ticker gate status (which gates are open, which are vetoed, and why), and portfolio exposure summary (current positions, Greeks, margin utilization). The pipeline pulls from DataFabric, runs the gate router, and formats the output as both plaintext (for terminal) and HTML (for email). It replaced a manual checklist that took 15 minutes each morning.

Data Refresh Pipeline

The refresh pipeline (scripts/refresh_pipeline.py) runs nightly at 6:00 PM ET after market close. It pulls the day’s OHLCV from yfinance, appends to per-ticker parquets, regenerates TFT predictions for all tickers with available models, updates the Chronos directional forecasts, and writes a manifest (artifacts/refresh_manifest.json) recording what was updated and whether any step failed. If the TFT prediction step fails, the gate router’s fallback logic ensures the next morning’s brief still runs with CMF gates. The pipeline is idempotent: running it twice on the same date produces identical output.

Test Coverage

The infrastructure push added 50+ tests across 8 test files. Key coverage areas: DataFabric lazy indicator correctness (verifying that computed RSI matches a reference implementation to 6 decimal places), gate router fallback logic (testing every combination of missing config, missing parquet, stale predictions), and morning brief formatting (asserting section ordering and VIX threshold triggers). The test suite runs in 14 seconds on CI, down from 45 seconds after removing redundant fixture rebuilds.

What V15 Taught Me

The week produced a paradox. The simplest signal in the system — Chaikin Money Flow (CMF), a volume-weighted accumulation/distribution indicator from the 1980s — still produces the highest Sharpe ratio of any single gate. CMF gate Sharpe: 1.71. TFT gate Sharpe: 1.53. Chronos gate Sharpe: 1.22. On risk-adjusted returns per trade, CMF wins.

But the TFT produces more portfolio PnL ($181K vs $165K) because it identifies more valid trading opportunities. CMF is conservative — it vetoes borderline regimes that the TFT correctly identifies as low-vol. The TFT’s lower Sharpe per trade is offset by higher trade volume in regimes where premium selling is profitable.

The Chronos walk-forward was a clean negative result. Foundation models trained on diverse time series do not transfer to financial vol prediction. Multivariate inputs made it worse. The univariate price-only model retains a marginal edge and stays in the stack as a directional filter, not a vol predictor.

Ensemble methods degraded every standalone model. The component errors are too correlated. Per-ticker dedicated models are the path — INTC’s 2.4x improvement from its own TFT validates the approach. The next phase will train dedicated models for TSLA, NVDA, and AMD, and measure whether the per-ticker Sharpe converges toward CMF while retaining the volume advantage.

The infrastructure work — DataFabric, gate routing, morning brief, refresh pipeline, 50+ tests — is invisible in a results table but is the reason the research stack can now run a full experiment cycle (data refresh, model prediction, gate decision, portfolio report) without manual intervention. That autonomy is the real product. The models are replaceable. The evaluation harness that tells you honestly whether a model works is not.

From Synthetic
to Live

Replacing Black-Scholes pricing with real option chains exposed how far theoretical models drift from executable reality

The champion scanner now prices every covered call recommendation against real bid/ask spreads, open interest, and market-implied Greeks. INTC’s theoretical $48.00 strike became a $49.50 real contract at $1.29 mark with 189 contracts of open interest. Every discrepancy matters when you are writing 160 contracts.

Stack$Trader Research TeamWHIPSAW  ·  March 2026
9 min read

All 24 SELL_CC positions generated by today's champion scanner are now priced from live Schwab option chains rather than Black-Scholes synthetics, and the differences are not trivial. INTC's scanner previously targeted a theoretical $48.00 strike at ~10 DTE with a $1.37 modelled premium; the real best-match contract is the $49.50 March 20 call at a $1.29 mark, with 189 contracts of open interest and a $0.08 bid-ask spread. The strike is $1.50 higher, the premium is $0.08 lower, and the DTE is one day shorter. Every one of those discrepancies matters when you are writing 160 contracts.

The Problem with Synthetic Pricing

Stack$Trader's champion scanner (scripts/champion_scanner.py) produces daily trade recommendations by passing the latest bar data through genetically evolved veto gate trees. When a tree evaluates to FALSE—permitting the covered call—the scanner must answer a concrete question: which contract, at what price? Until today, the answer came from Black-Scholes.

The theoretical path works like this. The engine's strike_for_delta() function inverts Black-Scholes to find the strike price that yields a target delta, typically 0.25 to 0.50 depending on the evolved bet_params. It then rounds that strike to the nearest standard grid increment ($0.50 below $50, $1.00 below $200, $5.00 above). Finally, bs_call() prices the option and the companion Greeks functions produce theta, gamma, and vega. It is elegant, internally consistent, and wrong in several quietly important ways.

First, the strike grid is an approximation. Real option chains do not follow uniform spacing; they cluster around the money and widen in the wings. A BS-implied $48.00 strike may not exist as a listed contract. Second, the volatility input is a single number—usually the ticker's historical realised vol or the VIX-derived implied vol from the bar data—whereas real contracts carry their own market-implied volatility that varies by strike and expiry along the surface. Third, and most critically for execution, a BS model says nothing about liquidity. A theoretical $1.37 premium is meaningless if the contract has three lots of open interest and a $0.40 bid-ask spread.

Architecture of the Integration

The integration adds three new functions to scripts/champion_scanner.py and modifies the existing compute_trade() into a dual-path dispatcher. The design principle is conservative: Schwab live data is the primary path, Black-Scholes is the fallback, and the caller never needs to know which path was taken.

Batch Chain Fetching

The new fetch_option_chains() function takes the list of SELL_CC scan results and batch-fetches real option chains through SchwabBroker.get_option_chain_live(). Each call requests 10 strikes around the money, filtered to CALL contracts within a DTE window centred on the champion's evolved dte_min parameter. Results are session-cached in a module-level _chain_cache dictionary, so repeated calls during the same run (across the trades and tradelist subcommands, for instance) do not generate redundant API traffic.

Rate limiting is handled with a 500ms sleep between calls. For 24 tickers, total fetch time is approximately 12 seconds—acceptable for a tool that runs once or twice per trading session. If the SchwabBroker is not configured (no API credentials), or if a specific ticker's chain fetch fails, the cache entry is set to None and the downstream code falls back to BS. This is not a hard dependency on the broker being available.

Contract Matching by Weighted Score

The core of the integration is match_best_contract(), which selects the single best real contract from a Schwab chain response. The algorithm iterates every contract in the callExpDateMap, applies a set of hard filters, and then scores the survivors by weighted distance from the target parameters.

The hard filters eliminate contracts with fewer than 5 DTE, non-positive delta, zero bid, strikes deeper than 10% in-the-money, and open interest below 10. These are not configurable; they represent fundamental tradability constraints.

The soft scoring function is:

score = 3.0 * |delta - target_delta| + 1.0 * |dte - dte_min| / max(dte_min, 1)

The 3:1 delta weighting reflects a deliberate priority: getting the right risk profile matters more than getting the exact expiration date. A contract that is 0.05 delta off-target at the right DTE scores worse than one that is 0.01 delta off-target but three days longer. This is grounded in the observation that theta decay is approximately linear in this part of the curve (9-37 DTE), so a few extra days cost relatively little, whereas delta misalignment directly affects the probability of assignment and the hedge ratio.

The lowest-scoring candidate wins. From it, the function extracts strike, DTE, expiry date string, delta, gamma, theta, vega, IV (normalised from Schwab's percentage format), bid, ask, mark, OI, and volume—a complete contract specification ready for the trade table.

The Dual-Path Dispatcher

The modified compute_trade() now accepts an optional chain parameter. When a chain is present, it calls match_best_contract(). If a valid match is returned, all pricing fields—strike, DTE, premium, Greeks—are populated from the real contract. If the chain is None or no match survives the filters, the function falls through to the original BS path unchanged.

A pricing_source field ("schwab" or "bs_theoretical") is attached to every trade result, and propagated to the output table as the Src column: S for Schwab, BS for Black-Scholes. In today's run, all 24 positions show S.

The real contract universe is both narrower and richer than the theoretical one. Narrower because listed strikes are discrete, not continuous. Richer because each strike carries its own implied volatility, its own liquidity profile, and its own market consensus about where the underlying is going.

What Changed in Practice

The first live run with Schwab data produced the tradelist at journal/tradelist/2026-03-11_1359_daily.md: 24 SELL_CC positions across the portfolio, 11 vetoed, 371 total contracts, $74,735 in premium, and $4,037.30 in daily theta. Every contract is now specified by its full OCC symbol (e.g., INTC 260320C00049500), expiration date, bid/ask/mark, and open interest.

Several patterns emerged immediately from the real-data output.

Strikes Shift Upward

BS-derived strikes tended to land on "nice" grid values that undershot the actual nearest listed OTM contract. INTC illustrates this most clearly: the theoretical path produced a $48.00 strike at delta 0.40, but the real chain's best match is $49.50—a full 3.1% OTM versus 0.04% OTM. The real contract is more conservative (lower assignment risk) but also offers less premium ($1.29 vs. $1.37). This is a real tradeoff that the BS path could not surface.

Greeks Shift with Market-Implied Vol

Because Schwab contracts carry their own implied volatility rather than the single historical-vol estimate used by BS, the Greeks change as well. INTC's theta moved from $1,680/day (BS at 58% vol) to $1,712/day (Schwab at 64% IV)—a modest 1.9% increase. But COIN shifted more meaningfully: Schwab reports 73% IV against the BS path's 68% estimate, pushing theta from $241/day to $257.50/day, a 6.8% gain. For a short-vol portfolio, these differences accumulate.

The Tradelist Command

The Schwab integration ships alongside a new tradelist subcommand for the champion scanner. Running python3 scripts/champion_scanner.py tradelist produces a complete markdown file at journal/tradelist/YYYY-MM-DD_HHMM_daily.md containing:

1. A signal summary table (24 SELL_CC, 11 VETO today).

2. A full trade table ranked by daily theta, with every column now populated from live data: expiry, bid, ask, mark, OI, and source indicator.

3. A portfolio summary: $74,735 total premium, $4,037.30/day theta, -16,062 share-equivalent delta, -1,636.82 gamma, -25.49 vega.

4. A vetoed positions table showing each ticker's current spot, veto rate, fitness, and tree.

5. Per-ticker strategy analysis sections that unpack each veto tree's logic stack, forward-test results, and the specific OCC contract symbol to trade.

The trade spec for each position now reads like an executable order. For VOO:

Sell 20x VOO   260327C00625000 exp 2026-03-27 (16 DTE)
Bid/Ask: $8.00 / $9.70 (mark $8.85, spread $1.70)
Premium: $17,700 | Theta: +$600.00/day | Delta: -886
Open Interest: 179 | Volume: 1

This is a format that could be piped directly into an order entry system with minimal transformation. The OCC symbol, expiry, and contract count are all present. The gap between signal generation and execution narrows considerably.

Limitations and What Remains Synthetic

Three caveats temper the improvement.

First, the chain data is a snapshot. By the time a human reviews the tradelist and places the order, bid-ask spreads will have moved. In volatile markets, the mark could shift by 5-10% in the minutes between scan and execution. The system does not yet implement real-time streaming quotes or limit-order placement.

Second, the Greeks reported by Schwab are themselves model-derived—Schwab runs its own pricing engine behind the API. The theta, gamma, and vega values are not "market-observed" in any direct sense; they are Schwab's model output parameterised by the contract's market-implied volatility. They are more accurate than our single-vol BS estimate, but they are still models.

Third, the open interest filter at 10 contracts is a blunt instrument. A contract with 15 OI might still be functionally illiquid for a 160-lot order (as in INTC). A more sophisticated approach would compare order size to OI and average daily volume, flagging positions where the order represents more than, say, 20% of daily volume. This is left for a future iteration.

The Wider Context: Evolution Meets Execution

Today's work sits at a junction point in the Stack$Trader architecture. The overnight and intraday veto evolution runs—launched earlier this morning with 10-hour and 8-hour budgets respectively—are discovering champion gate trees that produce SELL_CC or VETO signals. Those signals are abstract until they can be mapped to real, tradable contracts. The Schwab integration closes that gap.

The daily evolution has completed pass 1 across 35 tickers (100% found positive-fitness champions, average delta-Sharpe +0.64) and is deep into pass 2. The intraday 5-minute evolution finished pass 1 across 41 tickers with a 2.9x higher average fitness (delta-Sharpe +1.84), likely driven by the 6x data density of 5-minute bars. AVWAP-derived triggers (avwap_sl_below, avwap_sh_near, avwap_sh_above) appear heavily in both daily and intraday champion trees, validating yesterday's statistical analysis at multiple resolutions.

Concurrently, the Numba-JIT engine (engine_numba.py, 1,321 LOC) landed today with compiled Black-Scholes pricing and bar-by-bar position management. Once validated, its 10-50x speedup on the inner backtest loop could enable running 10x more generations per evolution budget—or, alternatively, incorporating the live chain matching into the evolution fitness function itself, pricing every candidate tree against real market data rather than synthetics.

That is the eventual convergence: genetically evolved signal logic, evaluated against live option chains, priced by a JIT-compiled engine, and presented as executable orders with full liquidity context. Today's integration is one piece of that stack, but it is the piece that touches reality.

The INTC Assignment Problem: From 50% Risk to Smart Consolidation Trading

20,000 shares, 4 immovable assignments, 200+ backtests, and the regime analysis that finally changed the question

Chris · March 29, 2026 · 12 min read

INTC is the largest single-stock position in the StackStrader portfolio at 20,000 shares, and it has the worst assignment record: 4 forced exercises that persist across every defensive configuration tested. Over the course of March 29, I ran more than 200 backtests spanning delta ranges, gate combinations, roll mechanics, and earnings avoidance. None of them eliminated those 4 assignments. The solution that finally worked did not try to survive the trending moves — it avoided writing calls during them entirely.

The Scale of the Problem

INTC’s 20,000-share position generates meaningful covered call (CC) premium — roughly $4,800 per cycle at the target delta. But each assignment costs approximately $440 in direct tax friction (10% of realized gain forced at an unfavorable time), plus the opportunity cost of re-entering the position at a higher basis. Four assignments per year on INTC alone costs $1,760 in tax drag, and that figure excludes the bid-ask spread on re-entry and the lost premium from weeks spent out of position.

The four problematic assignments share a common profile: INTC moves 10%+ in a single week, blowing through any strike the system could have selected at any reasonable delta. These are not marginal in-the-money expirations — they are full trending breakouts where the stock closes $3–5 above the strike.

Everything That Failed

The first sweep on March 28 tested delta ranges from 0.08 to 0.25, but produced suspiciously uniform results. Root cause: the config parameter was named target_delta, but the engine consumed delta. The value never propagated — every run used the default. Commit bc510100 corrected this and re-ran the full matrix.

Delta Reduction

Lowering target delta from 0.16 to 0.08 moves the strike further out of the money. For INTC, this means roughly $1.50 more headroom per cycle. The four trending moves exceed $5 in a week. Delta reduction buys insufficient room.

Gate Combinations

Tested every available gate: CMF momentum, Hurst exponent, ATR volatility, RSI, MACD divergence, and all pairwise combinations. The champion gate — AND(cmf_positive, NOT(hurst_accel)), commit 1ac25196 — improved portfolio-level PnL by 14% and reduced total assignments from 19 to 14. But INTC’s 4 assignments survived every gate. The gates correctly block marginal trades on other tickers; INTC’s trending moves are not marginal.

Earnings-Aware Early Close

Commit f687b7bf added an earnings_close_days parameter: buy back open CCs N days before an earnings announcement. Tested with values of 1, 2, 3, and 5 days. Result: INTC’s 4 assignments are not earnings-driven. They occur during non-earnings trending weeks — foundry news, sector rotation, and macro-driven rallies that have nothing to do with the quarterly reporting calendar.

Roll-Up-and-Out

The roll-up-and-out logic in engine_numba.py had a critical bug: it was rolling to the same expiry at a higher strike. A real roll should find the next available expiry beyond the current one, then select the appropriate strike. Commit f687b7bf fixed this. The corrected logic fired 9 rolls across the backtest period. Zero assignments were prevented. When INTC is trending 10%+ in a week, rolling to a strike $2–3 higher with one more week of time does not help.

The VetoGateAlpha Inversion

While debugging why earn_avoid was not protecting against earnings assignments, I discovered that all human-readable triggers work backwards when routed through VetoGateAlpha. The inversion: VetoGateAlpha treats trigger=True as “veto this trade.” So earn_avoid (True when earnings are near) was correctly vetoing from the engine’s perspective — but from a human perspective, it blocked non-earnings trades and allowed earnings trades.

The full scope of the inversion:

  • earn_avoid — blocks when earnings are NOT near, allows when they are
  • cmf_positive — blocks when CMF IS positive, allows when negative
  • Every human-readable trigger has this same inversion

The V12 GA-evolved triggers were designed with this inversion already baked in — the GA optimized trigger polarity to maximize fitness, so it naturally discovered the correct (inverted) polarity. Human-readable triggers added since March 21 were written with natural-language semantics, which is exactly backwards through the VetoGateAlpha path. The fix: wrap in NOT(). Semantically ugly, mechanically correct.

Regime Analysis: 49.6% Baseline Assignment Probability

The dedicated INTC TFT model (commit 827683e, March 28) enabled a proper regime analysis. INTC’s annualized realized volatility is 56% — placing it permanently in the high-vol regime for a covered call strategy. The baseline assignment probability, computed as the fraction of CC cycles where the stock closes above the strike at expiry, is 49.6%.

INTC’s 49.6% assignment rate is higher than TSLA’s despite lower absolute vol because INTC’s moves are more directional. TSLA whipsaws — it gaps up 8% then drops 6% within the same cycle, often finishing near the strike. INTC trends: when it moves, it moves in one direction for the entire expiry cycle. Directional persistence, not absolute volatility, drives assignment risk.

The TFT model catches 2 of the 3 major trending moves in its vol spike predictions. But catching the move and preventing the assignment are different problems. By the time the vol spike is detectable, the CC is already open and deep in the money.

The assignment problem is not episodic. INTC is permanently in the high-vol regime. The question is not “when will assignments stop?” but “when is it safe to write calls?”

The Solution: Consolidation Trigger

Commit dc63df0d introduced intc_consolidation_safe, a trigger that reframes the question. Instead of asking “can we survive this move?” it asks “is the stock likely to make a large move right now?”

The trigger logic has two components:

1. Rally Confirmation

Require the stock to have risen at least 5% over the past 20 trading days. This confirms that upside has already been captured — the rally that would cause an assignment has already happened. Writing a CC after a rally profits from the elevated implied volatility while the directional move is exhausted.

2. Consolidation Detection

Require 5 consecutive days where the daily range (high minus low) is below the 20-day Average True Range (ATR). This confirms the move has cooled. Consolidation is the empirical signature of a trend pause — the stock is digesting gains, not extending them.

3. Strike Ceiling

Cap the strike at min(52-week high, spot + 10%). This prevents the system from writing calls that would be exercised if the stock merely returns to its recent high. The 10% cap provides a hard ceiling on the maximum upside surrender.

The Combined Effect

The trigger only permits CC writing when all three conditions are met: a recent rally (elevated IV, exhausted trend), consolidation (reduced directional risk), and a protected strike (capped upside exposure). This combination targets the specific market microstructure that precedes safe CC writing on high-vol names.

Results

Assignment probability dropped from 45.5% to 23.1% in backtesting — roughly halved. The remaining 23.1% represents cycles where the stock breaks out of consolidation into a new trend. These are genuinely unpredictable events; no trigger can eliminate them without also eliminating all premium income.

The Sharpe ratio improves from 0.71 to 0.94 because assignment losses are the primary source of variance.

Rust Backend Parity

Commit fb093542 ported the earnings-aware early close and roll-up-and-out fix to the Rust backend. Earlier in the day, I flagged the growing feature divergence between the Rust and Numba engines as a maintenance risk — tests that ran against the default Rust backend silently skipped new features. The port resolves this for the two most critical features.

The Rust engine now consumes the same earnings_days array and earnings_close_days parameter as the Numba backend. The roll logic correctly advances to the next available expiry rather than rolling to the same expiry at a higher strike. Both backends produce identical results on the INTC test suite.

Honest Conclusions

The consolidation trigger halves INTC’s assignment risk. It does not eliminate it. A 23.1% assignment rate means roughly 1 in 4 CC cycles still results in exercise. On a stock with 56% annualized realized volatility, this is close to the theoretical floor — you cannot write covered calls on a high-vol name without accepting some assignment risk. The premium exists precisely because the risk exists.

The broader lesson from this investigation: reactive defenses (rolls, early close, delta reduction) fail against large trending moves because they activate too late. The only effective defense is not being in the trade during the dangerous period. The consolidation trigger achieves this by identifying safe windows rather than trying to survive dangerous ones.

Whether this approach generalizes beyond INTC depends on whether other high-vol tickers share the same rally-then-consolidation pattern. TSLA’s whipsaw character suggests it may not. The next step is per-ticker consolidation analysis across the full portfolio, with ticker-specific calibration of the rally threshold and consolidation window.

Evidence
and Illusion

A field guide to quantitative finance methodology, the researchers who built it, and the traps that swallow everyone else

Five decades of serious empirical finance have produced a rigorous methodology for distinguishing genuine edge from data-mined illusion. Most practitioners know the vocabulary. Far fewer apply the discipline consistently. This is a complete account of what the field has learned — the tools, the traps, the strategies that endured, and the researchers whose work earned permanent authority.

The EditorsWHIPSAW  ·  March 2026
20 min read

In 1974, Fischer Black published a short paper in the Journal of Finance titled "The Pricing of Commodity Contracts." It contained the Black formula for futures options. Three years earlier he and Myron Scholes had published the options pricing model that would bear their names. Both papers were rejected by journals on first submission — the Journal of Finance turned down Black-Scholes twice before the Journal of Political Economy accepted it in 1973. The rejection letters cited the work's departure from standard economic assumptions. The editors were wrong. But their caution was not unreasonable: financial markets are awash with patterns that appear real in limited samples and dissolve on examination. The history of quantitative finance is the history of developing tools precise enough to tell the difference.

This article surveys that history from the practitioner's vantage point: what the core methodological tools are and how they work, who built them and why their authority is earned, which strategies have demonstrated genuine persistence across regimes and markets, and what structural traps exist between a backtest that looks good and a strategy that actually is good. A bibliography at the end provides the primary sources.

I The Foundation: What a Backtest Actually Tests

The Fundamental Contamination Problem

A backtest is a counterfactual simulation: given the strategy rules as specified, what would have happened if those rules had been applied to historical data? This question has a definitive answer, and the answer is almost always positive, for a simple reason: the rules were derived by examining the data. The act of specification — choosing indicators, parameters, holding periods, position sizing — is itself a form of fitting to historical patterns. Every decision the researcher makes is informed, consciously or not, by having seen the outcomes.

Andrew Lo and Archie MacKinlay named the most common form of this problem in their 1990 paper "Data Snooping Biases in Tests of Financial Asset Pricing Models": if you search a dataset long enough with enough hypotheses, you will find patterns. The patterns are real — they exist in the data. They are just not informative about the future. Lo and MacKinlay showed that standard statistical tests applied to trading rules exhibit dramatically inflated rejection rates when the rules were derived from the same data used for testing. Their work established the theoretical basis for why naive backtesting is insufficient and why the entire edifice of modern backtesting methodology exists to address this problem.

The Out-of-Sample Imperative

The only principled response to the contamination problem is temporal separation: establish the strategy rules entirely on data from one period, then test on data from a different period that was not used in rule derivation. The holdout data — typically called the out-of-sample set — must be genuinely unseen. Using it to make any decision about the strategy, even a minor parameter adjustment, contaminates it.

In-sample vs. out-of-sample

In academic finance, "in-sample" refers to the data used to estimate a model. "Out-of-sample" refers to data not used in estimation, used to test generalisability. The terms are sometimes used loosely; always clarify which period was used for model development before interpreting a reported performance figure.

Walk-forward validation extends this principle to rolling windows. Rather than a single train/test split, the data is divided into N folds. The model is trained on folds 1 through k, tested on fold k+1, then trained on folds 1 through k+1 and tested on fold k+2, and so on. Each fold contributes exactly one out-of-sample period to the overall performance record. The Walk-Forward Efficiency ratio — OOS Sharpe divided by IS Sharpe — measures how much of the in-sample performance survives to the out-of-sample period. WFE significantly below 1.0 indicates overfitting; WFE near 1.0 indicates the strategy generalises cleanly; WFE above 1.0, paradoxically, indicates that the structural edge is so robust that the parameter-fitting process during optimisation actually hurt in-sample returns by selecting configurations that were too conservative.

II The Sharpe Ratio: Uses and Abuses

William Sharpe introduced his reward-to-variability ratio in 1966, in a paper comparing the performance of mutual fund managers. The measure — the ratio of excess return to standard deviation of returns — became the dominant risk-adjusted performance metric in finance, a status it retains today despite well-documented limitations.

Sharpe Ratio — Sharpe (1966), modified formulation SR = (R̄p − Rf) / σp Annualised: SR_annual = SR_period × √(periods per year) Daily → Annual: SR × √252 Monthly → Annual: SR × √12 R̄p = mean strategy return per period  |  Rf = risk-free rate
σp = standard deviation of strategy returns  |  The annualisation formula assumes i.i.d. returns — an assumption that is almost always violated.

The Sharpe ratio's limitations are structural, not incidental. It treats upside and downside volatility symmetrically — a strategy with large positive outliers is penalised as heavily as one with large negative outliers, even though only the latter represents genuine risk. It assumes returns are normally distributed; for options strategies with skewed or fat-tailed distributions, the Sharpe ratio systematically misstates the risk-return profile. And its annualisation — multiplying by √(periods per year) — assumes independent, identically distributed returns, an assumption violated by any strategy with autocorrelated performance.

Bailey and López de Prado (2012) formalised the Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio — the probability that the true Sharpe ratio exceeds a benchmark given finite observations — which correctly accounts for the uncertainty in Sharpe ratio estimates from limited samples. Their key finding: a Sharpe of 1.0 estimated from 100 monthly observations has a 95% confidence interval of roughly [0.4, 1.6]. The single-number Sharpe is a point estimate of a highly uncertain quantity. Reporting it without a confidence interval understates the estimation error substantially.

Practical Sharpe ratio hygiene requires: reporting the number of independent trades or periods from which it was estimated; reporting maximum drawdown and Calmar ratio (annualised return divided by maximum drawdown) alongside it; distinguishing clearly between in-sample and out-of-sample Sharpe figures; and noting whether the strategy's return distribution is approximately normal or whether the Sharpe ratio is therefore a questionable summary statistic.

III Permutation Testing: The Correct Null

Parametric statistical tests — t-tests, F-tests, confidence intervals — require assumptions about the distributional form of the data. Financial returns violate nearly all of these assumptions: they are fat-tailed, autocorrelated, heteroskedastic, and non-stationary. Permutation tests avoid these assumptions entirely by constructing an empirical null distribution directly from the data.

The logic is straightforward. If the ordering of returns carries no information — if the strategy's performance is entirely attributable to which days it happened to be in the market rather than which days it chose to be in the market — then randomly shuffling the order of those returns should produce performance indistinguishable from the observed performance. Compute the Sharpe ratio of 10,000 shuffled versions of the return series. Count how often the shuffled Sharpe exceeds the observed Sharpe. That fraction is the permutation p-value.

The Masters Correction for Structural Strategies

Timothy Masters, in his 1995 book Testing and Tuning Market Trading Systems, identified a critical flaw in naive permutation tests applied to strategies with structural return components: shuffling the entire return series preserves any structural component that is order-independent. For a covered call strategy, theta decay accrues positively every day regardless of the order in which the position was entered. Shuffling entry dates shuffles which theta values are collected, but since theta is always positive, the shuffled series produces nearly the same Sharpe as the observed series. The p-value inflates toward 1.0 not because the strategy lacks edge but because the null distribution has the same structural return as the observation.

Masters Path-Dependent Permutation — decompose, then permute the residual r_t = θ_t + α_t (decompose into structural + timing components) H₀ : α_t is symmetric around zero (timing adds no value) Permutation: for i = 1…N, draw signs_i ∈ {−1, +1}^T independently r_perm_i = θ + signs_i ⊙ α (sign-flip α, hold θ fixed) p = (1/N) · #{i : Sharpe(r_perm_i) ≥ Sharpe(r_obs)} θ_t = structural component (theta accrual, carry, dividend) — computed from first principles
α_t = residual (entry timing, selection, sizing decisions) — what we are testing
The null is correctly centred on structural-only performance, not on zero.

The Masters correction generalises: for any strategy with a structural return component, decompose the returns into that component and the residual, then permute only the residual. This applies to carry strategies (carry return is structural; timing is the residual), dividend capture strategies, and any mean-reversion strategy with a known theoretical reversion speed.

White's Reality Check and Hansen's SPA

When a backtest involves not one strategy but many — a grid search across parameter combinations, or a systematic scan of factors — the multiple comparison problem requires correction. Halbert White's 2000 paper "A Reality Check for Data Snooping" provided the first rigorous bootstrap test correcting for data snooping across multiple trading rules simultaneously. The test constructs a joint null distribution across all M strategies by resampling returns with replacement, then evaluates whether the best-performing strategy's excess returns are statistically significant after accounting for the fact that M strategies were tested.

White's Reality Check is known to be conservative when many of the M strategies perform poorly — poor strategies inflate the null distribution, making it harder for genuinely good strategies to clear the significance bar. Peter Hansen's 2005 paper "A Test for Superior Predictive Ability" corrected this weakness by studentising each strategy's statistic by its HAC-consistent standard error before computing the test statistic, making each strategy's contribution proportional to its statistical reliability rather than its raw underperformance. Hansen's SPA test is the current standard for rigorous multiple-strategy significance testing in systematic finance.

V The Researchers Who Built the Field
Eugene Fama
University of Chicago  ·  Nobel 2013

Established the Efficient Market Hypothesis in its three forms (1970). The EMH is not a claim that markets are perfect; it is a claim that price reflects available information rapidly enough to make consistent excess returns extremely difficult to achieve. Fama's subsequent work with Kenneth French (1992, 1993, 2015) documented the three-factor and five-factor models, identifying size and value as systematic risk factors that explain equity return differences. His insistence on controlling for data snooping and publishing null results alongside positive ones set methodological standards that much of the field has failed to match.

Campbell Harvey
Duke University  ·  Former AFA President

Harvey, Liu, and Zhu's 2016 paper ". . . and the Cross-Section of Expected Returns" is arguably the most important methodological contribution in finance in decades. They surveyed 316 published factors, showed that the t-statistic required to claim statistical significance in factor research should be at least 3.0 (not the conventional 2.0) due to community-level data snooping, and concluded that roughly half of all published factors are likely false positives. Harvey has since documented that factor returns decay significantly post-publication, consistent with arbitrage and the backfill of data-mined results.

Andrew Lo
MIT Sloan  ·  Principal, AlphaSimplex

The Adaptive Market Hypothesis (2004) reframes market efficiency as an evolutionary process: market participants adapt, strategies decay as competition increases, and new opportunities arise as the environment changes. Lo's framework explains why strategies work for a while and then stop: they are correct responses to a given market ecology, not permanent features of price dynamics. His work on hedge fund performance and serial correlation in returns identified systematic biases in reported fund performance. Jointly with MacKinlay, established the foundational theory of data snooping biases in financial tests.

Cliff Asness
AQR Capital Management  ·  PhD Chicago

Asness's 1994 dissertation documented the coexistence of momentum and value — the finding that both factors work simultaneously despite pointing in opposite directions in the short term, and that combining them improves risk-adjusted performance. His subsequent work at AQR on value, momentum, carry, and defensive strategies across asset classes (with Moskowitz, Pedersen, and others) established the multi-asset factor framework that underpins modern systematic investing. The "Quality Minus Junk" factor (2019) extended the analysis to profitability and earnings quality as return predictors.

Robert Shiller
Yale  ·  Nobel 2013

Shiller's demonstration of excess volatility in stock markets (1981) — that stock prices fluctuate far more than the present value of dividends can justify — was among the earliest rigorous evidence against the strong form of market efficiency. His Cyclically Adjusted P/E (CAPE) ratio remains one of the most empirically robust long-horizon valuation predictors. Shiller's work grounded the case for mean-reversion in valuations over decade-long horizons — the mechanism behind the value premium — without requiring irrational behaviour, only slow information incorporation and time-varying risk premia.

Fischer Black
Goldman Sachs  ·  MIT

Beyond the Black-Scholes model (with Scholes, 1973), Black's intellectual contributions to systematic trading methodology are underappreciated. His 1972 paper with Jensen and Scholes on the low-beta anomaly was among the first documented departures from the CAPM. His work on the zero-beta CAPM established the theoretical framework for understanding why risk-adjusted returns do not simply scale with beta. Black's intellectual legacy is methodological rigour applied to empirical anomalies — always asking whether the result could have a risk-based explanation before invoking market irrationality.

Peter Carr & Liuren Wu
NYU Tandon / Baruch College

Carr and Wu's 2009 paper "Variance Risk Premiums" provided the first systematic cross-asset documentation of the volatility risk premium — the persistent gap between implied and realised volatility — across equity indices, individual stocks, and currency pairs. Their risk-neutral measure framework established why the VRP should exist as structural compensation for variance uncertainty, not as a market anomaly. The paper underpins the theoretical case for systematic premium-selling strategies and is the canonical academic reference for the structural edge in covered call and short-put programs.

Timothy Masters
Independent researcher & author

Masters' 1995 book Testing and Tuning Market Trading Systems remains the practitioner's definitive reference on statistically rigorous backtesting. His path-dependent permutation methodology correctly handles strategies with structural return components — carry, theta, dividends — where naive shuffling produces an inappropriate null distribution. His walk-forward framework and insistence on understanding what a test is actually measuring, versus what the researcher hopes it measures, established the standard that separates professional systematic research from backtesting theatre. Little-cited in academic finance; widely used by serious practitioners.

VI Strategies That Endured

The following represents the short list of strategies for which the empirical evidence, across multiple decades, multiple asset classes, multiple research groups, and realistic transaction cost assumptions, has proven sufficiently robust to warrant serious treatment. Each has a plausible risk-based or structural explanation; none is a free lunch.

Value
Fama-French 1992
Long cheap assets (low P/B, P/E, EV/EBITDA), short expensive ones. Five decades of equity data, cross-asset evidence in Asness et al. (2013). Works because cheap assets are genuinely riskier — distressed companies, out-of-favour sectors — or because mean-reversion in valuations is a slow, painful process that most investors cannot hold through. Decayed in the 2010s during growth/tech dominance; rebounded 2022+. The premium compresses during momentum bull markets and recovers during corrections.

Long high-carry assets, short low-carry, where carry is defined as the return if prices stay constant (yield differential in FX, roll yield in commodities, coupon minus funding in fixed income). Documented across equity indices, currencies, bonds, and commodities. Earns a crash risk premium — carry strategies are exposed to "carry crash" events when risk appetite reverses suddenly. The AMP (2013) paper showed carry's Sharpe near 0.7 across asset classes before transaction costs.

Low-Volatility
Black-Jensen-Scholes 1972
Low-beta / low-volatility assets outperform high-beta assets on a risk-adjusted basis — a direct violation of the CAPM prediction that expected return scales linearly with beta. The anomaly persists because leverage-constrained investors (mutual funds, pension funds) reach for high-beta stocks to boost returns, driving up their prices and reducing their forward returns. Baker, Bradley, and Wurgler (2011) documented it as a robust, multi-decade anomaly across global equity markets.
Endures

Carr-Wu 2009
Implied volatility persistently exceeds realised volatility, providing a structural premium to sellers of optionality. Documented across equity indices, individual stocks, currencies, and commodities. The premium is compensation for variance uncertainty — buyers of insurance pay above actuarially fair price for the certainty of the hedge. Whaley (2002) documented the long-term BXM (buy-write) index performance; Israelov and Nielsen (2015) decomposed covered call returns into equity, short-vol, and uncompensated reversal components.

Quality / Profitability
Novy-Marx 2013 / AQR 2019
High-profitability, high-earnings-quality firms outperform low-quality firms. Novy-Marx (2013) showed gross profitability predicts cross-sectional returns with roughly the same power as book-to-market. Asness et al.'s "Quality Minus Junk" (2019) formalised a composite quality factor. Combines powerfully with value — cheap + quality is more persistent than either alone.

1-week losers outperform 1-week winners. Documented historically, driven by market microstructure — liquidity providers extracting short-term premiums, order flow imbalances correcting. VII Structural Traps and How They Destroy Strategies

The biases discussed in Section IV are methodological errors — they arise from mistakes in research process and can be eliminated with discipline. The traps in this section are structural: they arise from the nature of financial markets and the interaction between strategies and the environments they operate in. They cannot be eliminated, only understood and managed.

Regime non-stationarity is the most important. Financial markets are not stationary processes — the statistical properties of returns change over time as the economic environment, regulatory structure, investor base, and technology evolve. A strategy that worked in a low-rate, low-volatility, post-GFC bull market environment may fail not because it was badly designed but because the regime in which it was designed no longer exists. The test for regime sensitivity is asking: what is the causal mechanism behind the edge, and is that mechanism dependent on the current regime? Value works because valuations mean-revert over decades — a mechanism robust to rate regimes. A strategy that profits from low-volatility trending markets has a mechanism directly exposed to the rate and macro regime that produced that environment.

The publication-decay problem is well-documented. McLean and Pontiff (2016) studied 97 published equity anomalies and found that out-of-sample (post-publication) returns declined by an average of 58% compared to the in-paper backtest period. Harvey (2020) showed that this decay is concentrated in anomalies with higher in-paper t-statistics — precisely the ones that look most convincing. The mechanism is straightforward: publication broadcasts the strategy, capital flows in, the price impact of that capital arbitrages away the excess return. The implication is that any strategy you can read about in an academic journal has already been substantially competed away.

"The best in-sample t-statistics are the most likely to decay post-publication. The publication process selects for the most overfitted results."

Harvey, Liu & Zhu — ". . . and the Cross-Section of Expected Returns" (2016)

Crowding and liquidity correlation represent a form of regime risk that is invisible in backtests. When many systematic strategies converge on the same positions — as they frequently do, since they read the same literature, use the same data, and apply the same optimisation frameworks — a correlated deleveraging event can produce losses that have no historical precedent in any individual strategy's backtest. The "quant quake" of August 2007 saw equity long-short momentum strategies lose 20-30% in a single week as quantitative funds simultaneously deleveraged. No individual strategy's backtest had ever shown returns of that magnitude, because the crowding was a new phenomenon that emerged only after enough capital had followed the same rules.

The capacity problem is perhaps the most underappreciated trap for individual researchers developing strategies on personal capital. A strategy that returns 40% annually on $100,000 might return 4% on $10 million, because market impact — the price movement caused by the act of trading — grows faster than linearly with position size in most markets. The backtest never models this because the backtest assumes zero market impact. When capacity is a binding constraint, apparent excess returns are partially a function of the small size at which the strategy was tested, not of the strategy's properties in general.

Parameter fragility is the clearest diagnostic of overfitting. A genuine structural edge is robust to reasonable parameter perturbation: if value works with P/B ratios, it should also work with P/E ratios and EV/EBITDA, because they are all measuring the same underlying phenomenon (relative cheapness) through different lenses. If a strategy only works at exactly RSI(14) and fails at RSI(12) and RSI(16), the edge is in the parameter number, not in the concept. Systematic parameter sensitivity testing — varying each numeric parameter ±20% and documenting how performance changes — is the most practical tool for detecting parameter-specific overfitting before it becomes a live trading problem.

VIII What Endures and Why

Across the strategies in Section VI, a common pattern emerges: the ones that endure are the ones that can be explained as compensation for bearing a risk that most investors prefer not to bear. This is not a coincidence. If a strategy earns excess returns without bearing any identifiable risk, rational investors should arbitrage it away — and they do. The strategies that persist do so precisely because they are unpleasant to hold.

Value investing requires holding assets that are out of favour, reporting poor earnings, carrying high financial risk. The performance drawdowns during value underperformance periods — 1998-2000, 2017-2020 — are long enough and severe enough that most institutional investors abandon the factor before it recovers. The investors who can hold through those periods collect the premium. The ones who cannot, don't.

Momentum requires holding last year's winners and shorting last year's losers with conviction, including when momentum crashes — sudden, severe reversals that occur when the market environment shifts rapidly. Momentum crashes are infrequent but devastating; a strategy's ability to harvest the momentum premium depends on institutional capacity to maintain conviction through those events, which most cannot.

The VRP requires selling insurance and bearing the consequent left-tail risk. The seller earns the premium in the 80% of periods when the insured event does not occur but bears catastrophic loss risk in the 20% when it does. Strategies that manage this left-tail exposure — through regime filtering, earnings avoidance, position sizing, and careful instrument selection — earn the premium more reliably than those that sell indiscriminately. But the premium exists because the exposure is real. There is no version of systematic premium selling that eliminates the left tail; there are only versions that price it correctly and manage it intelligently.

Lo's Adaptive Market Hypothesis is perhaps the most honest framework for thinking about strategy longevity. Strategies are adaptive responses to a market ecology at a given point in time. As more capital flows into the same strategy, the ecology adapts: prices adjust, the strategy becomes less profitable, and eventually a new opportunity emerges elsewhere. The question is not "will this strategy work forever?" but "what is the causal mechanism, how robust is it to the current ecology, and how will I know when the ecology has changed?"

Foundations of Market Efficiency and Factor Research

Fama, E. F. (1970). Efficient capital markets: A review of theory and empirical work. Journal of Finance, 25(2), 383–417. The canonical statement of the EMH in weak, semi-strong, and strong forms. Essential reading for understanding what "market efficiency" actually claims.

Fama, E. F., & French, K. R. (1992). The cross-section of expected stock returns. Journal of Finance, 47(2), 427–465. Established the size and value factors as systematic return predictors. The foundational paper for factor-based investing.

Fama, E. F., & French, K. R. (2015). A five-factor asset pricing model. Journal of Financial Economics, 116(1), 1–22. Extended the three-factor model with profitability and investment factors. Current standard for multi-factor equity return attribution.

Shiller, R. J. (1981). Do stock prices move too much to be justified by subsequent changes in dividends? American Economic Review, 71(3), 421–436. First rigorous documentation of excess volatility in equity prices relative to fundamental value.

Black, F., Jensen, M. C., & Scholes, M. (1972). The capital asset pricing model: Some empirical tests. In M. C. Jensen (Ed.), Studies in the Theory of Capital Markets (pp. 79–121). Praeger. First documented the low-beta anomaly — a persistent departure from CAPM predictions that remains unexplained by standard risk models.

Momentum, Reversal, and Return Predictability

Jegadeesh, N., & Titman, S. (1993). Returns to buying winners and selling losers: Implications for stock market efficiency. Journal of Finance, 48(1), 65–91. The definitive documentation of cross-sectional momentum. One of the most cited papers in empirical finance.

Jegadeesh, N. (1990). Evidence of predictable behavior of security returns. Journal of Finance, 45(3), 881–898. Short-term return reversal at monthly horizons — the precursor to momentum research.

Asness, C. S., Moskowitz, T. J., & Pedersen, L. H. (2013). Value and momentum everywhere. Journal of Finance, 68(3), 929–985. Cross-asset documentation of value and momentum across equities, bonds, currencies, and commodities. Established the diversification benefit of combining the two factors.

Daniel, K., & Moskowitz, T. J. (2016). Momentum crashes. Journal of Financial Economics, 122(2), 221–247. Documented the left-tail risk of momentum strategies — infrequent but severe crashes that explain why the premium persists despite widespread awareness.

Ball, R., & Brown, P. (1968). An empirical evaluation of accounting income numbers. Journal of Accounting Research, 6(2), 159–178. The original documentation of post-earnings announcement drift. One of the earliest market anomalies; has substantially decayed in recent decades.

Volatility, Options, and the Risk Premium

Black, F., & Scholes, M. (1973). The pricing of options and corporate liabilities. Journal of Political Economy, 81(3), 637–654. The foundational options pricing model. Rejected twice before publication; awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in 1997 (Black had died in 1995).

Carr, P., & Wu, L. (2009). Variance risk premiums. Review of Financial Studies, 22(3), 1311–1341. First systematic cross-asset documentation of the volatility risk premium. The canonical academic reference for premium-selling strategy structural foundations.

Whaley, R. E. (2002). Return and risk of CBOE buy write monthly index. Journal of Derivatives, 10(2), 35–42. Long-term BXM (BuyWrite) Index performance analysis. Established the empirical benchmark for systematic covered call strategies.

Israelov, R., & Nielsen, L. N. (2015). Covered calls uncovered. Financial Analysts Journal, 71(6), 44–57. Decomposed covered call returns into equity, short-vol (VRP), and uncompensated reversal components. Proposed a risk-managed variant hedging the reversal exposure.

Coval, J. D., & Shumway, T. (2001). Expected option returns. Journal of Finance, 56(3), 983–1009. Documented that options earn returns inconsistent with standard risk models, providing early evidence for the VRP as a distinct risk premium.

Statistical Methodology and Testing

Lo, A. W., & MacKinlay, A. C. (1990). Data-snooping biases in tests of financial asset pricing models. Review of Financial Studies, 3(3), 431–467. Established the theoretical framework for data snooping bias. The foundational justification for out-of-sample testing and multiple comparison correction.

White, H. (2000). A reality check for data snooping. Econometrica, 68(5), 1097–1126. Bootstrap-based Reality Check test correcting for multiple strategy comparisons. Conservative but honest; widely used in financial backtesting.

Hansen, P. R. (2005). A test for superior predictive ability. Journal of Business & Economic Statistics, 23(4), 365–380. Superior Predictive Ability test — corrects White's RC conservatism by studentising strategy statistics. The current standard for multi-strategy significance testing.

Harvey, C. R., Liu, Y., & Zhu, H. (2016). . . . and the cross-section of expected returns. Review of Financial Studies, 29(1), 5–68. McLean, R. D., & Pontiff, J. (2016). Does academic research destroy stock return predictability? Journal of Finance, 71(1), 5–32. Documented 58% average decay in anomaly returns post-publication. Publication-driven arbitrage is a quantifiable phenomenon.

Market Microstructure and Adaptive Markets

Lo, A. W. (2004). The adaptive markets hypothesis: Market efficiency from an evolutionary perspective. Journal of Portfolio Management, 30(5), 15–29. Documented overnight/intraday return decomposition and cross-period reversal. Provides the theoretical framework for overnight risk management in systematic strategies.

Baker, M., Bradley, B., & Wurgler, J. (2011). Benchmarks as limits to arbitrage: Understanding the low-volatility anomaly. Financial Analysts Journal, 67(1), 40–54. Documented the low-volatility anomaly robustly across global markets and explained it via leverage-constrained benchmark-relative investors.

Novy-Marx, R. (2013). The other side of value: The gross profitability premium. Journal of Financial Economics, 108(1), 1–28. Established gross profitability as a cross-sectional return predictor with power comparable to book-to-market. Foundational for the quality factor.

Asness, C., Frazzini, A., Israel, R., Moskowitz, T., & Pedersen, L. (2018). Size matters, if you control your junk. Journal of Financial Economics, 129(3), 479–509. Showed that the size premium is robust once controlling for quality — cheap-and-quality small-cap stocks are the primary source of the premium.
